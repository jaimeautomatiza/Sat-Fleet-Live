What Is SatFleet Live?

SatFleet Live is a free, real-time satellite tracking platform that shows the live position of every active satellite currently orbiting Earth — over 15,000 of them — on an interactive world map or photorealistic 3D globe. It runs entirely in your browser with no downloads, no account, and no cost. There is also a free Android app available on Google Play for an optimised mobile experience with push notifications.

Unlike most satellite trackers that show only a handful of famous objects, SatFleet Live displays every single active, operational satellite with current orbital data: GPS and navigation constellations, the International Space Station, China's Tiangong, the entire Starlink fleet, weather satellites, military spacecraft, Earth observation satellites, amateur radio cubesats, and thousands more. Space debris and defunct hardware are deliberately excluded — every dot you see is a functioning piece of hardware in orbit right now.

But SatFleet Live is far more than a satellite map. It integrates live rocket launch tracking with embedded YouTube streams, a Next Passes predictor that calculates exactly when any satellite will fly over your GPS location, a compass-based AR Sky View for your phone, and detailed per-satellite information pages pulling data from NORAD, Wikidata, and Wikipedia. Everything updates continuously — satellite positions recalculate every second; rocket launch data every 5–15 minutes depending on how close to launch you are.

The Live Satellite Map (2D)

The default view when you open SatFleet Live is the interactive 3D globe, powered by CesiumJS with ESRI World Imagery. You can switch to the 2D flat map at any time using the 2D button in the top bar. Each satellite appears as a small colour-coded dot — its colour indicates its category (blue for GPS, white for space stations, green for Starlink, and so on). Positions update every second.

Clicking a satellite

Tap or click any dot to open the satellite info panel. It shows the satellite's name, current altitude in kilometres, velocity in km/h, and live latitude and longitude coordinates — all recalculating in real time. The map simultaneously draws the satellite's predicted orbital path for the next ~100 minutes as a colour-matched dashed line. Click the satellite's name in the panel to open its full detail page.

Your location marker

With location permission granted, SatFleet Live places a red marker on the map at your current GPS position. On mobile devices with a working magnetometer, the marker becomes a directional arrow that rotates as you turn — useful for lining up what you see on the map with the actual sky. Your coordinates are stored only in your browser's localStorage and never leave your device.

Clicking on empty space

Clicking anywhere on the map that isn't a satellite automatically closes any open info panel, removes the orbital track, and animates the camera back to the default global view — keeping the map clean and uncluttered.

3D Globe — See Every Orbit in Perspective

Press the 3D button in the top bar to switch to the interactive globe, powered by CesiumJS with ESRI World Imagery as the base layer. Earth renders as a photorealistic sphere with country borders, atmospheric haze, and real satellite photography at up to zoom level 17.

Satellites appear as colour-coded points floating above the globe at their correct altitudes. The ISS at 400 km skims just above the surface. GPS satellites at 20,200 km form a distant shell far above. Geostationary satellites at 35,786 km sit in a thin ring at the equator, barely moving. This altitude perspective — impossible to convey on a flat map — makes orbital mechanics immediately intuitive.

⚡ Performance

The 3D globe uses a dedicated Web Worker running in a background thread to calculate all 15,000+ satellite positions every 200 milliseconds. The main thread only handles rendering — it never stalls. Between worker updates, the globe interpolates each satellite's position frame by frame using linear interpolation, producing fluid real-time animation even for 15,000 simultaneous objects.

360° POV mode

Select any satellite and press the 360° button (bottom-right) to lock the camera to that satellite and orbit around it. Drag to rotate your viewpoint, pinch or scroll to zoom in and out. This mode is also available for rocket launch pads — select a rocket and press 360° to view the pad from any angle at ground level.

3D compass

On mobile devices with an active compass, a small compass rose appears in the bottom corner of the 3D globe, rotating as you move your phone. This is useful for correlating what you see on the globe with your actual orientation outdoors.

How It Works: The Technology Behind It

Understanding how SatFleet Live works helps explain both its accuracy and its speed. The platform is built on three interlocking layers: a data pipeline that fetches and caches orbital data, a physics engine that propagates orbits in real time, and a rendering layer that visualises everything on screen.

📡
Step 1: TLE Data from CelesTrak / NORAD Every satellite's trajectory is encoded in a Two-Line Element set (TLE) — a standardised two-line string of numbers that captures the orbital parameters: inclination, eccentricity, semi-major axis, right ascension, argument of perigee, mean anomaly, and mean motion. TLEs are published by the US Space Force / NORAD and distributed by CelesTrak. SatFleet Live fetches fresh TLE data for all 15,000+ active satellites every 12 hours through its own Cloudflare Worker proxy.
⚙️
Step 2: SGP4 Propagation in a Web Worker TLE data alone tells you where a satellite was at a reference time. To find where it is right now, you run the SGP4 (Simplified General Perturbations Model 4) algorithm — the industry standard for LEO and MEO satellites. SatFleet Live runs SGP4 on all 15,000 satellites simultaneously inside a JavaScript Web Worker (a background thread), recalculating positions every 200 milliseconds. The main thread — which handles the 3D globe rendering — is never blocked. The worker sends a shared Float64 buffer of positions back to the main thread, which then interpolates each satellite's movement between updates for completely smooth animation.
🌐
Step 3: Rendering with CesiumJS & Leaflet In 3D mode, satellite positions are mapped to CesiumJS PointPrimitives — a batch rendering approach that draws all 15,000+ points in a single GPU draw call, making the globe fast even on mid-range devices. In 2D mode, Leaflet Circle Markers are used. Both modes receive position updates from the Web Worker and move the markers accordingly, with the 3D globe additionally performing linear interpolation every frame for fluid motion between worker updates.
🚀
Step 4: Launch Data from The Space Devs API Rocket launch information comes from the Launch Library 2 API by The Space Devs, served through SatFleet Live's own Cloudflare Worker with an adaptive KV cache. The cache refreshes every 5 minutes when a launch is within 6 hours, every 15 minutes otherwise — minimising API calls while keeping countdown timers accurate. Push notifications for imminent launches (T−5 minutes) are dispatched automatically when the Worker detects a launch is about to happen.
Open-source libraries used:

satellite.js (SGP4/SDP4 propagator) · CesiumJS 1.114 (3D globe) · Leaflet.js (2D map) · ESRI World Imagery (satellite textures) · CartoDB Dark (2D basemap) · Cloudflare Workers + KV (backend caching) · OneSignal (push notifications)

Only Visible — What You Can Actually See Tonight

Press Only Visible in the top bar to filter the entire map down to satellites that are genuinely observable with the naked eye from your location at this exact moment. Three conditions must be simultaneously true:

  1. Above your horizon — The satellite's elevation must exceed the minimum you set (default 10°, adjustable up to 90° with a slider). Satellites near the horizon are often blocked by trees, buildings, or atmospheric haze.
  2. Dark enough sky — The Sun must be at least 6° below your horizon (nautical twilight or darker). During daylight, even bright satellites are invisible against the blue sky.
  3. Satellite in sunlight — The satellite itself must still be illuminated by the Sun — not in Earth's shadow. SatFleet Live calculates this using a precise ECI (Earth-Centred Inertial) shadow cone model.

This combination is most productive during the 30–90 minutes after sunset or before sunrise — the "golden window" when your sky is dark but sunlight still reaches low Earth orbit. At true midnight in summer, few LEO satellites remain sunlit over equatorial regions; at midnight in winter at high latitudes, the geometry improves significantly.

🔭 Pro tip: best conditions for satellite spotting

Set minimum elevation to 20°–30° to filter out low-angle satellites that are hardest to see, and focus on GPS (brightest of the navigation sats), ISS/Space Station (magnitude −3 to −4, clearly visible), and Weather/Meteorological satellites (often bright). The ISS is the brightest satellite in the sky — brighter than Venus — and passes over every location on Earth multiple times per week.

Next Passes — Predict Satellite Overheads

The Next Passes feature is SatFleet Live's most powerful tool for outdoor observers. It calculates exactly when any satellite category will fly over your specific GPS location over the next 1–7 days, including the precise start time, peak elevation, direction, duration, and estimated naked-eye brightness (apparent magnitude).

How the calculation works

The browser iterates over every satellite in the selected category, running SGP4 propagation in 2-minute steps across the prediction window. When a pass is detected (all three visibility conditions met), it refines the start and end times to 10-second precision by bisection. The peak elevation is then further refined in 10-second steps around the midpoint. All calculations happen in your browser — no server involved — typically completing within a few seconds for standard categories, or 10–30 seconds for "All satellites."

Reading a pass card

Each result card shows: start time (when the satellite clears 10° elevation), maximum elevation in degrees (90° = directly overhead; anything above 40° is easily visible), direction as three compass points (e.g. "SW → S → SE"), visible period from rise to set, and brightness in apparent magnitude with a label (Very Bright, Bright, Normal, Dim). Each card includes a mini Leaflet map showing the satellite's trajectory over your area during the pass.

Starlink train detection

The Next Passes algorithm automatically identifies Starlink trains — groups of recently launched Starlink satellites that pass in close succession, creating a moving chain of bright dots across the sky. When 3 or more Starlink satellites pass within 10 minutes of each other from a similar direction (within 45° azimuth), they are grouped under a highlighted "Starlink Train" header with the count and earliest start time.

Pass alerts & push notifications

Click Notify me on any pass card to schedule an alert. On the website, this uses browser notifications (the tab must stay open). On the Android app, tapping "Notify me" schedules a real push notification via OneSignal — delivered even when the app is closed — at T−10 minutes and T−2 minutes before the pass begins, with the satellite name, maximum elevation, direction, and brightness in the notification body.

Custom observation location

If your GPS isn't available or you want predictions for a different city, click Change location to open a mini Leaflet map. Click anywhere on the map or drag the marker to set your observation point. You can also tap "Use my GPS" to re-detect your current position.

Live Rocket Launches & Countdowns LIVE

SatFleet Live integrates rocket launch data from the Launch Library 2 API (The Space Devs) to show every upcoming launch directly on the map and globe, with live countdown timers, pad markers, and embedded live streams when a rocket is in the air.

The launches widget

A collapsible widget in the top-right corner shows the next upcoming launch with a live countdown. Expand it to see all launches in the next few days — mission name, vehicle, launch provider, pad location, status badge (GO / TBC / TBD / LIVE), and a T-minus or T-plus timer. If multiple launches are LIVE simultaneously, a selector lets you switch between them without leaving the page. The dedicated Launches page shows the full list with mission images and descriptions.

Rocket markers on the globe

Each upcoming launch appears as a rocket icon on the map and 3D globe at its exact launch pad coordinates. Icons are differentiated by provider — SpaceX Falcon icons look different from Rocket Lab Electron, Soyuz, Long March, Ariane, and others. Click any rocket marker to open a panel showing the mission name, countdown, pad location, and a link to the full launch page. Imminent launches are sorted to the front and drawn on top of other markers.

LIVE detection logic

A launch transitions to LIVE status — shown with a pulsing red LIVE badge and animated glow — the moment any of these occur: the API sets webcast_live: true, the status changes to "In Flight," or the status is "Go" and the scheduled launch window has already opened (within a 3-hour window). The live state persists until the API officially marks the mission as Success or Failure — so a long orbital insertion burn keeps the rocket visible on the map throughout.

Embedded live streams

When a launch is LIVE and the API provides a YouTube stream URL, a click-to-play thumbnail appears inside the launches widget. Clicking it replaces the thumbnail with an embedded YouTube player that auto-starts. On mobile, a Fullscreen button appears below the player. Collapsing the widget stops the stream and restores the thumbnail — no background audio surprises. If multiple simultaneous live launches have streams, you can switch between them with the inline selector.

Rocket category filters

The Layers panel includes rocket sub-filters: All, 🔴 Live, Today, SpaceX, Rocket Lab, Soyuz, China, Europe, ULA / Blue Origin, Other. Combine any rocket filter with any satellite filter, or disable the entire Rockets layer to leave a clean satellite-only view.

AR Sky View — Satellites on Your Camera

Available at satfleetlive.com/ar.html and through the Android app, the AR Sky View is a mobile-first feature that overlays real satellite positions on the sky using your phone's compass, accelerometer, and optionally camera.

Compass mode vs Camera mode

In Compass Mode (default), satellites are plotted on a dark sky canvas with elevation rings, cardinal direction labels, and a crosshair showing where you're pointing. No camera permission needed. Switch to Camera Mode using the "Camera" button to activate the rear camera — the canvas becomes semi-transparent, overlaying satellite positions on your actual camera feed for true augmented reality.

How the positioning works

The phone's DeviceOrientationEvent provides heading (compass bearing), tilt (elevation angle), and roll (rotation). SatFleet Live reads these values 60 times per second, applies a 4-sample circular moving average to smooth compass jitter, and recalculates which satellites should appear within the current 80° × 70° field of view. Satellites outside the frame are hidden; those inside are plotted at their correct angular position based on azimuth and elevation.

Filters in AR

A Filters panel in AR mode mirrors the main map categories — restrict the view to Space Station only (great for ISS spotting), GPS, Starlink, Military, Weather, or any combination. The Only Visible (night) option applies the same three-condition check — above horizon, dark sky, satellite in sunlight — leaving only genuinely observable satellites on screen.

Tapping satellites in AR

Tap any satellite dot to open a popup showing its name, altitude, velocity, elevation angle, and azimuth. A dashed orbital path also appears on screen, showing where the satellite has come from and is heading. Tap anywhere else to deselect and close the popup.

🧭 Calibration tip

Compass accuracy varies by device and environment. If satellites appear misaligned with the real sky, move your phone through a figure-8 motion several times to recalibrate the magnetometer. Metal objects, speakers, and cases with magnets can cause compass drift — try removing your phone from its case if alignment is off.

Satellite Search by Name or NORAD ID

The search box in the top bar accepts both satellite names and NORAD Catalog Numbers. Type "ISS" to jump directly to the International Space Station, or type "25544" (the ISS's NORAD ID) for the same result. Partial name matching works — "Starlink" matches the first Starlink satellite in the dataset; "Hubble" finds the Hubble Space Telescope instantly.

The search also covers rocket launches. Typing a mission name (e.g. "Crew-9"), vehicle name ("Falcon 9"), or pad name ("Vandenberg") will find and fly the camera to the matching launch pad and open its info panel.

When a satellite is found, the map or globe flies to its current position, opens the info panel with live data, and draws the orbital track — regardless of whether that satellite type is currently active in the filter settings. Search always bypasses filters.

Data Sources & Accuracy

🛰️
Satellite orbital data — CelesTrak / NORAD TLE data for all active satellites, refreshed every 12 hours via SatFleet Live's own Cloudflare Worker proxy (which caches and serves TLEs from a GitHub-hosted JSON mirror). The SGP4 model then provides position accuracy within a few kilometres for most LEO satellites. Accuracy degrades if a satellite has recently manoeuvred — a new TLE is typically published within 24–48 hours of any manoeuvre.
🚀
Rocket launch data — The Space Devs / Launch Library 2 Served through SatFleet Live's Cloudflare Worker with adaptive KV caching: every 5 minutes when a launch is LIVE or within 6 hours; every 15 minutes otherwise. Push notifications for T−5 minutes are dispatched by the Worker automatically using its scheduled Cron trigger.
📍
Your location — Browser Geolocation API Requested with your explicit permission. Stored only in localStorage on your device — never sent to any SatFleet server. A cached location loads instantly; GPS updates silently in the background.
☀️
Sun position — Astronomical algorithm Calculated client-side using Julian Day + ecliptic longitude, accurate to within ±1° for current dates. Powers the "Only Visible" filter, the AR visibility check, and Next Passes illumination logic.
📖
Satellite information — Wikidata + Wikipedia Queried via SPARQL for each satellite's NORAD ID, with a Wikipedia summary API call if an English article exists. Results are cached in Cloudflare KV for 7 days per satellite to minimise load on the Wikidata query service.
