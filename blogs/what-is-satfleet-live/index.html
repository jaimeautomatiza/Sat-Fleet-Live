If you've ever looked up at the night sky and wondered which of those moving dots are satellites — or wanted to know exactly when the ISS will pass over your house — SatFleet Live is the tool for you. It is a free, browser-based satellite tracker that shows the live position of over 14,000 active satellites in real time, with no downloads, no subscriptions, and no account required.

But SatFleet Live is more than just a map of dots. It includes a 3D globe powered by CesiumJS, a rocket launch tracker with live countdown timers and YouTube streams, a next-pass predictor for your exact GPS location, and even an experimental augmented reality sky viewer you can point at the sky with your phone. This guide walks through every feature, explains how it all works, and shows you how to get the most out of it.

What Is SatFleet Live?

SatFleet Live is a real-time satellite tracking website built entirely in the browser. There is nothing to install — you open it in Chrome, Firefox, Safari, or Edge and you immediately see thousands of satellites moving across a world map, updated every second.

Under the hood, SatFleet Live pulls orbital data in TLE (Two-Line Element) format from CelesTrak / NORAD — the universal standard for satellite position data, used by space agencies worldwide. It then runs those numbers through the SGP4 orbital mechanics model directly in your browser using the satellite.js library, computing each satellite's exact position in real time. No server-side processing, no delays — the calculations happen on your device.

The Live Satellite Map (2D)

The default view when you open SatFleet Live is the interactive 2D world map, built on Leaflet.js. Each satellite appears as a colour-coded dot moving in real time across the map — positions are updated every second using SGP4 calculations.

The map comes in a dark theme by default, using CartoDB's dark basemap, which makes the coloured satellite dots easy to read at a glance. You can switch to a light map using the Theme button in the top bar.

Clicking a satellite

Tap or click any dot on the map to open the satellite info panel. It shows the satellite's name, current altitude (in km), velocity (in km/h), and live latitude and longitude coordinates. The map also draws the satellite's predicted orbit path for the next ~100 minutes as a dashed line. Click the satellite's name in the panel to open its detail page with more historical and technical information.

📍 Your location on the map

SatFleet Live places a marker on the map at your GPS location (with your permission). On devices with a working compass, the marker becomes a directional arrow that rotates as you move — useful for outdoor observations. Your location is used to calculate satellite visibility and next passes, and is stored only in your browser's local storage.

3D Globe Mode

Press the 3D button in the top bar to switch to the interactive globe, powered by CesiumJS with ESRI satellite imagery. The globe renders Earth as it actually looks from space — with real photographic textures, country borders, and atmospheric effects.

In 3D mode, satellites appear as colour-coded points floating above the globe at their correct altitudes. You can rotate and zoom freely with mouse or touch gestures. The satellites update their positions every 2 seconds, and labels appear when you zoom in close enough. Clicking a satellite opens the same info panel as in 2D mode, and also draws the 3D orbital path as a dashed line curved around the globe.

Why 3D is different

In 2D, a GPS satellite at 20,200 km and an ISS at 400 km look identical on the map — both are just dots. In 3D, the altitude difference is immediately obvious: you can see the ISS skimming just above the surface while GPS satellites hover in a distant shell and geostationary satellites sit at a dramatically higher orbit far from Earth. It makes the geometry of space actually tangible.

🧭 3D compass

In 3D mode with an active compass (mobile devices), a small compass rose appears in the corner showing your current heading — a useful reference when cross-referencing what you see on the globe with the real sky above you.

Satellite Categories & Filters

With 14,000+ satellites on screen at once, filters are essential. Click the Layers button to open the filter panel. Satellites are divided into two main layers — Satellites and Rockets — each with their own sub-filters.

Each satellite category has a unique colour so you can identify groups instantly on the map:

By default, SatFleet Live loads GPS, Space Station, Communications, Military, Weather, Geodetic, and Technology satellites. Starlink and "Other" categories are off by default to avoid overloading the map — switching Starlink on will add over 9,000 dots at once (a warning dialog confirms before proceeding).

You can activate multiple categories simultaneously and combine them with the visibility filter. Switching off the Satellites layer entirely hides all satellites and leaves only rocket markers on the map.

Only Visible — See What's Above You Tonight

Press Only Visible in the top bar to filter satellites down to only those that are actually observable from your location at this moment. A satellite is considered visible when three conditions are met simultaneously: it is above your horizon (by at least the minimum elevation you set), the sky at your location is dark enough (the Sun is more than 6° below your horizon), and the satellite itself is still lit by sunlight — meaning it is above Earth's shadow cone.

The minimum elevation slider lets you raise the cutoff from 0° (anything above the horizon) to 90° (directly overhead). Raising it to 20–30° gives more realistic results, since low-elevation satellites are often obscured by buildings, trees, and atmospheric haze.

🔭 When to use "Only Visible"

This mode is most useful during astronomical twilight — roughly 30–90 minutes after sunset or before sunrise. At that time, your sky is dark enough to see satellites, but sunlight still reaches satellites orbiting at 400–600 km. During full daylight, almost nothing passes the visibility check. At midnight, nothing illuminated by the Sun passes over equatorial regions, so the filter may show very few results.

Next Passes — Predict Satellite Overheads

The Next Passes page calculates exactly when specific satellites will fly over your location over the next 1–7 days, including the precise start time, maximum elevation, direction (azimuth), and estimated visual brightness (apparent magnitude).

How it works

Click "Calculate Passes," select how many days and which satellite category you want, and SatFleet Live runs SGP4 calculations for every satellite in that category — checking every 2-minute window for visibility. It then refines each pass with 10-second resolution, so start and end times are accurate to within a few seconds. The full calculation runs entirely in your browser with no server calls.

What the results tell you

Each pass card shows the start time (when the satellite rises above 10°), the maximum elevation (the highest point it will reach — higher means brighter and easier to see), the direction expressed as three compass points (rising from → peaking at → setting to), and a brightness estimate in apparent magnitude (negative numbers are brighter; the ISS routinely reaches −3 to −4, brighter than anything in the night sky except the Moon).

Starlink train detection

Next Passes automatically detects Starlink trains — clusters of recently launched Starlink satellites passing in close succession. When three or more Starlink satellites pass within 10 minutes of each other from the same general direction, they are grouped under a "Starlink Train" header with a count and a link to the spotting guide.

Pass alerts

Click the Notify me button on any pass card to schedule browser notifications — 10 minutes before and 2 minutes before the pass. Notifications require permission and need the browser tab to stay open in the background. Enable them site-wide using the 🔔 button in the header, then set individual alerts per pass.

Custom location

If your GPS isn't available or you want to calculate passes for a different city, click Change location on the Next Passes page to open a mini Leaflet map. Click anywhere on the map or drag the marker to set your observation point. You can also tap "Use my GPS" to re-detect your position.

Rocket Launches & Live Tracking LIVE

SatFleet Live integrates launch data from the Launch Library 2 API (The Space Devs) to show upcoming and recent rocket launches directly on the map, complete with countdown timers and live streams.

The launches widget

A collapsible widget on the map shows the next upcoming launch with a live countdown. Expand it to see all launches in the next few days, each with the mission name, vehicle, launch pad, status badge (GO / TBC / LIVE), and countdown. Click any card to go to the full launches page.

Rocket markers on the map

Each upcoming launch appears as a rocket icon on the map at its launch pad location. Icons are colour-coded by provider — SpaceX Falcon rockets look different from Soyuz or Long March or Electron. The marker size and z-ordering prioritise imminent launches so they are never buried under other markers.

LIVE detection logic

A rocket switches to LIVE status — shown with a pulsing red badge and glow animation — when any of these conditions are met: the API reports webcast_live: true, the launch status changes to "In Flight," or the status is "Go" and the scheduled launch time has already passed (within a 3-hour window). The LIVE state persists as long as those conditions hold, regardless of how many hours have elapsed — so a long ascent or orbital insertion burn keeps the rocket visible on the map until the API reports Success or Failure.

Embedded live streams

When a launch is LIVE and there is a YouTube stream URL available from the API, the launch card in the widget displays a click-to-play thumbnail. Clicking it replaces the thumbnail with an embedded YouTube iframe that auto-plays the stream. On mobile, a Fullscreen button appears below the player. Collapsing the widget stops the stream and restores the thumbnail to avoid background audio.

Filtering rocket markers

The Layers panel includes rocket sub-filters: All, 🔴 Live, Today, SpaceX, Rocket Lab, Soyuz, China, Europe, ULA / Blue Origin, and Other. You can combine rocket filters with satellite filters, or turn off the entire Rockets layer with the Rockets toggle button.

🗺️ Flying to a launch pad from the Launches page

On the dedicated Launches page, each card has a "View on map" button. Clicking it stores the pad coordinates in sessionStorage and redirects to the main map, which then flies the camera to that exact location and opens the rocket's info panel automatically — useful for zooming in to watch a live launch in real time.

AR Sky View (Beta)

Available at satfleetlive.com/ar.html, the AR Sky View is a mobile-first tool that overlays real satellite positions on your sky using your phone's compass and accelerometer. Point your device at any part of the sky and coloured dots appear where satellites currently are — in real time.

Two modes

In Compass Mode (the default), satellites are plotted on a dark sky canvas with cardinal directions and elevation rings. This works without the camera. Switch to Camera Mode using the "Camera" button to activate your rear camera — the canvas becomes semi-transparent, overlaying satellite positions on your actual camera feed for a true augmented reality experience.

Calibration

Compass-based AR requires a calibrated magnetometer. If satellites appear misaligned, a calibration modal explains how to move your phone in a figure-8 motion to recalibrate. A toast notification appears if no compass data is detected within 1.5 seconds of loading.

Filters in AR

A Filters button in AR mode opens the same category panel as the main map — you can restrict the view to Space Station only (great for ISS spotting), GPS, Starlink, or any other category. The "Only Visible (night)" filter applies the same three-condition visibility check as the main map.

Tapping satellites

Tap any satellite dot in AR mode to open a popup showing its name, altitude, velocity, elevation angle, and azimuth. A dashed orbital path also appears on the sky canvas showing where the satellite is heading.

Satellite Search by Name or NORAD ID

The search box in the top bar accepts both satellite names and NORAD IDs. Type "ISS" to jump to the International Space Station, or type "25544" (the ISS's NORAD Catalog Number) for the same result. Partial matches work — typing "Starlink" will match the first Starlink satellite it finds.

When a satellite is found, the map flies to its current position and opens the info panel. If the satellite isn't currently within the active filters, the search still finds and focuses it. The search also covers rocket launches — typing a rocket name, vehicle, or pad name will find and fly to a matching launch.

Data Sources & Accuracy

🛰️
Satellite orbital data TLE (Two-Line Element) data from CelesTrak / NORAD, served via SatFleet's own API worker and refreshed every 48 hours. The SGP4/SDP4 propagation model computes live positions to within a few km for most LEO satellites. For satellites that recently manoeuvred (orbital boosts, collision avoidance), accuracy degrades until a fresh TLE is published.
🚀
Rocket launch data Launch Library 2 API by The Space Devs. SatFleet Live caches results locally and uses an adaptive TTL — refreshing every 5 minutes when a launch is within 6 hours, every 10 minutes when within 24 hours, and every 15 minutes otherwise. This minimises API calls while keeping countdown timers accurate.
📍
Your location Obtained via the browser's Geolocation API (GPS or network triangulation) with your explicit permission. Stored only in your browser's localStorage — never sent to any server. A cached location from a previous session is loaded instantly on page open, while GPS updates in the background.
☀️
Sun position Calculated in real time using a standard astronomical algorithm (Julian Day + ecliptic longitude), not fetched from any API. This powers the "Only Visible" filter, visibility twilight checks, and the AR sky illumination model. Accuracy is within ±1° for current dates.
